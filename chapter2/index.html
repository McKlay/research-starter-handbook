    Chapter 2: Types of CS Research Topics¶

    ‚ÄúNot all research is built the same. Some prove theorems, some build systems, some ask questions no one thought to ask.‚Äù

    Why This Chapter Matters¶

    Computer Science is not a single discipline‚Äîit‚Äôs a constellation of subfields, each with its own values, methods, and outputs.

    As you begin to explore research, it‚Äôs easy to feel overwhelmed by the sheer variety of topics and approaches. Some students gravitate toward coding and building, while others are drawn to abstract puzzles or real-world impact. Understanding the landscape early helps you make informed choices and avoid frustration. The more you know about the different types of research, the better you can align your work with your strengths and passions.

    Some researchers build software or hardware systems. Others write mathematical proofs. Some conduct user studies; others train deep learning models. All of them are doing ‚Äúresearch,‚Äù but what they do day-to-day is vastly different.

    This chapter will help you map the landscape of CS research categories so you can ask:
    What kind of builder do I want to be?

    Understanding these research types early will save you from wasted time chasing projects that don‚Äôt fit your interests, skills, or goals.

    Conceptual Breakdown¶

    üîπ Systems vs. Theory vs. Empirical Research

    At a high level, CS research can be grouped into three big families:

    Let‚Äôs look closer at what these families mean in practice:

    • Systems research is hands-on and creative. You might design a new database, build a robotics platform, or develop a novel networking protocol. The work is often collaborative, involving teams and iterative testing. Success is measured by whether your system works, scales, and solves real problems.

    • Theory research is about deep thinking and abstraction. You‚Äôll spend time with mathematical proofs, logic, and complexity. The work is solitary at times, but also involves lively debates and peer review. Success is measured by the elegance and rigor of your results, and their impact on how we understand computation.

    • Empirical research is investigative. You‚Äôll design experiments, collect data, and analyze results. This could mean running user studies, benchmarking algorithms, or evaluating new technologies in the field. Success is measured by the quality of your evidence and the insights you uncover about real-world behavior.

    Type What It Focuses On Common Output
    Systems Building working software/hardware Frameworks, tools, protocols, datasets
    Theory Proving things mathematically Lemmas, theorems, complexity classes
    Empirical Testing and observing real-world behavior Experiments, user studies, evaluations

    Each has a different culture of proof, writing, and publication.

    For example, a systems paper might include code and performance benchmarks, while a theory paper focuses on definitions and proofs. Empirical work often features charts, statistics, and discussion of limitations. Learning to recognize these cultures will help you read papers more effectively and communicate your own work.

    üîπ Applied vs. Fundamental vs. Exploratory

    This is another lens to examine your direction:

    These categories are not rigid boxes. Many projects blend applied, fundamental, and exploratory elements. For instance, you might start with an exploratory idea, develop a fundamental understanding, and then apply it to solve a real problem. The key is to notice which questions excite you most‚Äîdo you want to build, understand, or discover?

    Type Key Question Example Topic
    Applied ‚ÄúHow can we use this to solve X?‚Äù Using AI to predict floods
    Fundamental ‚ÄúWhat are the limits or properties of Y?‚Äù Proving limits of cryptographic hash
    Exploratory ‚ÄúWhat happens if we try Z?‚Äù Visualizing large language model errors

    You can mix and match. Some applied work has theoretical backing. Some exploratory work becomes the seed of a new applied method.

    üîπ Quantitative vs. Qualitative Methods

    CS isn't only about numbers.

    • Quantitative research includes benchmarks, metrics, statistical tests, ML model performance, etc.

    • Qualitative research includes interviews, thematic analysis, user feedback, open coding, etc.

    HCI (Human-Computer Interaction), ICT4D (Tech for Development), and EdTech often use both.

    Choosing your method depends on your research question. If you want to measure how fast an algorithm runs, you‚Äôll use quantitative methods. If you want to understand how people feel about a new technology, qualitative methods are essential. Many impactful projects combine both, using numbers to show what‚Äôs happening and stories to explain why.

    Don‚Äôt assume ‚Äúreal research‚Äù only means code and math. If your research deals with people, policy, or perception‚Äîqualitative work is not just valid, it‚Äôs critical.

    üîπ Social Good and Interdisciplinary Research

    Some of the most impactful CS work lies at the intersection of disciplines:

    • AI + Law ‚Üí Legal tech, case triage, transparency bots
    • CS + Agriculture ‚Üí Smart irrigation, drone surveillance, soil health analysis
    • CS + Education ‚Üí Intelligent tutors, learning analytics
    • CS + Government ‚Üí Budget bots, policy dashboards, fraud detection

    These projects often:

    • Use applied methods
    • Require collaboration with domain experts
    • Involve both technical and human/systemic thinking

    Interdisciplinary research is challenging but rewarding. You‚Äôll need to learn the language of other fields, build relationships, and adapt your methods. The payoff is the chance to make a real difference in society. If you‚Äôre passionate about a cause‚Äîeducation, justice, health‚Äîconsider how CS can amplify your impact.

    This is where you come in. Your domain interests (education, justice, media, health, etc.) can drive the tech you build.

    Self-Check Questions¶

    1. Do you enjoy building and debugging systems? (You may lean toward Systems research.)

    2. Do you love math, proofs, and abstract thinking? (You might be drawn to Theory.)

    3. Do you prefer testing, analyzing, and validating behaviors or ideas? (Empirical work may suit you.)

    4. Do you care about solving real-world problems or building tools for people? (Consider applied and interdisciplinary research.)

    5. Do you see your research as a way to serve a community or cause? (Look into social good and policy-related research.)

    Reflect on your answers and consider discussing them with a mentor or peer. Sometimes, talking through your interests helps clarify your direction. Remember, your preferences may change as you gain experience‚Äîstay open to new possibilities.

    Researcher‚Äôs Compass¶

    Choosing a topic is only half the question. The other half is:
    What kind of work are you excited to do every day?

    Some researchers build. Others analyze. Some test. Some prove.
    You don‚Äôt need to do it all.

    The best research is the kind that keeps you engaged and motivated. Pay attention to what energizes you‚Äîwhether it‚Äôs coding, writing, experimenting, or collaborating. Your compass is your curiosity. Let it guide you toward projects that feel meaningful and sustainable.

    Remember, the journey of research is as important as the destination. Stay curious, keep exploring, and don‚Äôt be afraid to change course as you learn more about yourself and the field.

    Find your mode of inquiry‚Äîthe method that makes you feel curious, engaged, and capable‚Äîand you‚Äôll be more likely to finish what you start.
